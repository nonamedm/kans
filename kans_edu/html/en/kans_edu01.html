  1. Ⅰ. Fundamentals of Radiation

    1. 1. Radiation

      A. Definition
      • Radiation is the process by which energy is emitted as particles or waves. This process involves energy being emitted when an unstable atom transfers energy to a stable atom.
      B. Kinds of Radiation
      • Radiation is classified into different categories in accordance with their ionization ability
        • Ionizing Radiation; alpha rays, beta rays, gamma rays and X-rays.
        • Non-ionizing Radiation; visible light and ultra violet rays; These are not strict forms of atomic radiation by definition.
      • Radiation is also classified into particle radiation, and electromagnetic radiation in accordance with their physical states.
      • Radiation is further characterized into natural radiation and artificialradiation in accordance with its origin.
        • Natural Radiation; Mostly originates from the radionuclide created during the formation of the earth, and some others such as cosmic rays, which originate from outside the solar system or space.
        • Artificial Radiation; Has some different origins; The fission of uranium or thorium in nuclear reactors forms a variety of fission products, some radioactive atoms are produced in particle accelerators, X-rays are generated from X-ray generators, and electron beams are generated from EB accelerators, etc
        • In sofar, as radionuclides are the same, the radiation patterns are also the same, regardless of the origin of the radionuclides.

      2. Atoms, The Atomic Nucleus

      A. Atoms
      • All materials consist of molecules, and the molecules consist of atoms. The nucleus is the core of the atom and electrons revolve around the nucleus.
    3. B. The Atomic Nucleus
      • It consists of protons and neutrons, and the number protons is the same as its atomic number.
        • In the nucleus, in addition to protons, there are neutrons of equal quantity, or more than the number of protons, and the mass of thenucleus is the sum of the number of the protons and neutrons.
      • Nuclide : A kind of nucleus taking into account the number of protons and neutrons.
      • Isotope, Radioisotope
        • Isotope; A nuclide with the same number of protons but a different number of neutrons.
        • Radioisotope; The isotope of a radioactive material.
        • Hydrogen atoms consist of one proton in the nucleus. However, there is another hydrogen atom which consists of one proton and two neutrons in the nucleus. This atom is unstable due to its excess neutrons, and it emits beta rays. Thus, it is a radioisotope of hydrogen.

      3. Radioactive Decay, Decay Modes

      A. Radioactive Decay
      • Alpha(α) Decay : The mother nuclide emits alpha particles(mass=4) transforming to a more stable daughter nuclide of which the mass is less than the mother by 4.
      • Beta(β) Decay : The mother nuclide emits a negatron(e-) or positron(e+) transforming to a more stable daughter nuclide of which the atomic number changes but the mass remains unchanged.
        • Negatron Emission;Occurs in nuclides having an excess number of neutrons compared to protons in the nucleus; neutron(n)→ proton(p) + negatron(e-) + antineutrino(ν)
        • Positron Emission;Occurs in nuclides not having an excess number of neutrons compared to protons in nucleus; proton(p)→ neutron(n) + positron(e+) + neutrino(ν).
      • Gamma(γ) emission: Radionuclides having higher energy levels emit gamma photons with the same atomic number however they are of a more stable state. No transformation occurs in this process.

      4. Radioactivity, Units of Radioactivity

      • Definition: The ability of radiation emissions by a radionuclide.
        • Radioactivity(A) = dN/dt, N; number of radionuclides, t; time (sec)
        • SI Unit of radioactivity; becquerel(Bq); 1 decay per sec(dps). 1 Bq= 1 dps, Classical unit; curie(Ci), 1 Ci= 3.7×1010 dps dps; disintegration per second

      6. Nuclear Fission, Nuclear Power Generation

      • Uranium-235 is a typical fissile nuclide. When it is concentrated to a certain extent as nuclear fuel, it can proceed with steady fission reactions by clashing with neutrons in a nuclear reactor, emitting radiation together with large amounts of heat. Nuclear power plants, like coal burning plants, produce electricity by boiling water and producing steam. This steam turns turbines to produce electricity. The difference is that nuclear plants do not burn coal or oil, however they use uranium fuel consisting of solid ceramic pellets contained in bundles. The process entails the splitting of uranium-235 atoms in a nuclear reactor.
